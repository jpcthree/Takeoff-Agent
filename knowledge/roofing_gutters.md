## Code Minimums

### Roofing Requirements (IRC Chapter 9)

| Requirement | Standard | Code Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum roof slope (asphalt shingles) | 2:12 with double underlayment, 4:12 normal | IRC R905.2.2 |
| Minimum roof slope (metal panels) | 3:12 standing seam, 1/4:12 with lapped seams | IRC R905.10 |
| Minimum roof slope (membrane/BUR) | 1/4:12 minimum | IRC R905.9-R905.13 |
| Underlayment required | Yes, all asphalt shingle roofs | IRC R905.1.1 |
| Ice barrier (Zones 5+) | Required at eaves, extending 24" past interior wall line | IRC R905.1.2 |
| Ice barrier in valleys | Required in all climate zones where ice barrier is required | IRC R905.1.2 |
| Wind speed compliance | Shingles rated for site wind speed | IRC R905.2.4.1 |
| Drip edge | Required at eaves and rakes | IRC R905.2.8.5 |
| Ventilation (vented attics) | 1:150 NFA ratio (1:300 with balanced intake/exhaust) | IRC R806.2 |
| Unvented roof assemblies | Air-impermeable insulation at sheathing, or conditioned attic | IRC R806.5 |

### Flat/Low-Slope Specific (< 2:12)

| Requirement | Standard | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Membrane type | TPO, EPDM, PVC, modified bitumen, BUR | Shingles NOT allowed below 2:12 |
| Drainage | Positive drainage required | 1/4" per foot minimum slope to drains |
| Parapet scuppers | Sized per roof area | Emergency overflow scuppers at 2" above primary drain |

## Best Practices

- **Ice and water shield at all penetrations** — Even in dry climates, ice and water shield at pipe boots, vents, skylights, and chimney bases prevents leaks from wind-driven rain and ice dams.
- **Kick-out flashing where roof meets sidewall** — The #1 source of water intrusion in residential construction. A $15 piece of flashing prevents $15,000 in rot repair. Always include even if not on plans.
- **Starter strip at eaves AND rakes** — Plans usually call for starter at eaves only. Adding starter at rakes seals the edge and improves wind resistance. ~$0.50/LF additional.
- **Proper step flashing at sidewalls** — Each piece of step flashing should be 2" wider than the shingle exposure. Woven step flashing (integrated with shingles) is superior to continuous L-metal.
- **Ventilation balance** — Intake (soffit vents) should equal or exceed exhaust (ridge vent). Unbalanced ventilation creates negative pressure and pulls conditioned air into the attic.
- **Nail placement in high-wind zones** — Shingles have a nailing line. 6 nails per shingle in areas with >110 mph design wind speed (standard is 4). This affects material quantities.
- **Drip edge over ice barrier at eaves, under at rakes** — The overlap direction prevents water from wicking behind the drip edge. Getting this backwards causes fascia rot.
- **Metal flashing at parapet copings** — Parapets are extremely vulnerable to water intrusion. Continuous metal coping with drip edges on both sides is standard. Screw-fastened with neoprene washers.

### Flat Roof Best Practices

- **Positive drainage to scuppers or internal drains** — Standing water is the enemy of flat roofs. Code requires positive drainage (1/4" per foot min). Tapered insulation or crickets create slope.
- **Emergency overflow scuppers** — Required 2" above the primary drainage level. Prevents structural overload from a clogged primary drain. Size at 1.5x the primary drain capacity.
- **Parapet flashing extends 8" minimum up the wall** — The base flashing at the parapet must extend high enough that standing water from a blocked drain can't overtop it.
- **Walkway pads over membrane** — Foot traffic on membrane roofing causes premature wear. Install walkway pads on service routes to HVAC equipment, drains, and access hatches.

## Common Alternatives

- **Architectural vs. 3-tab shingles**: Architectural (dimensional) shingles are now the industry standard. Thicker, better wind rating (130 mph vs. 60 mph), longer warranty (30-50 yr vs. 20-25 yr). Cost impact: +$15-20/square over 3-tab. Best for: any pitched roof project. Trade-off: none — 3-tab is being phased out.
- **Synthetic vs. felt underlayment**: Synthetic is lighter, stronger, more UV-resistant (can be exposed for months vs. days for felt). Cost impact: +$5-8/square. Best for: any project where roof won't be completed in a single day. Trade-off: felt is cheaper if budget is critical.
- **Standing seam metal vs. shingles**: 50+ year lifespan, no fastener penetrations, excellent for low slopes. Cost impact: 2-3x the cost of architectural shingles. Best for: modern design, low-slope applications, hail-prone areas (Denver). Trade-off: cost, expansion noise.
- **TPO vs. EPDM membrane (flat roofs)**: TPO is white (reflective, energy efficient), heat-welded seams (stronger). EPDM is black (absorbs heat, good for cold climates to melt snow), glued seams. Cost impact: similar ($4-7/SF installed). Best for: TPO in hot climates, EPDM in cold if snow-melt is desired. Trade-off: TPO has shorter track record than EPDM.
- **Modified bitumen vs. TPO/EPDM**: Torch-applied or self-adhered. Multi-ply for redundancy. More forgiving of imperfect installation. Cost impact: comparable to TPO. Best for: complex roof geometry, residential flat roofs. Trade-off: torch application is a fire risk during installation.

## Climate Zone Considerations

### Zone 5 (Cold — Denver)

- **Ice barrier required at eaves** — Must extend 24" past the interior wall line. In Denver, ice dams are less common than in humid-cold zones, but code still requires it.
- **Hail considerations** — Denver/Front Range is one of the highest-hail-risk areas in the US. Impact-resistant shingles (Class 4, UL 2218) earn insurance discounts of 15-30% and are worth suggesting on every estimate. Cost impact: +$20-30/square.
- **UV exposure at altitude** — Higher UV degrades roofing faster. Premium shingles with SBS-modified asphalt perform better than standard oxidized-asphalt shingles. Standing seam metal is UV-immune.
- **Freeze-thaw on flat roofs** — Ponding water + freeze cycles stress membrane seams. Positive drainage is critical. Tapered insulation systems are worth the investment.
- **Snow load** — Denver ground snow load is 30 PSF (IRC Table R301.2). Flat roofs must handle drifting at parapets — drift loads can be 2-3x ground load. Structural, not roofing scope, but the roofing system must accommodate the drainage of snowmelt.
- **Metal expansion** — Denver's 30-40F daily temperature swings cause significant expansion/contraction in metal coping, flashing, and gutters. Use expansion joints every 20-30 LF in metal runs.

### Zone 2-3 (Hot — Houston, Phoenix)

- **Cool roof requirements** — Many jurisdictions require minimum solar reflectance. White TPO or cool-roof-rated shingles may be required.
- **Wind resistance** — Gulf Coast and hurricane zones require higher wind ratings (150+ mph). Enhanced nailing patterns and adhesive-strip shingles.

### Zone 6-7 (Very Cold — Minneapolis)

- **Ice barrier is critical** — Full ice barrier from eave to 3+ feet past interior wall line. Consider full-deck ice barrier on low-slope sections.
- **Ventilation even more important** — Warm attic + cold exterior = ice dams. Balanced ventilation prevents warm spots on the roof deck.

## Gutter System Knowledge

### Sizing Guidelines

| Roof Area Served | Gutter Size | Downspout Size | Max Run Between Downspouts |
|---|---|---|---|
| < 600 SF | 5" K-style or half-round | 2x3 | 40 ft |
| 600-1000 SF | 5" K-style or 6" half-round | 3x4 | 50 ft |
| > 1000 SF | 6" K-style or box | 3x4 or 4x4 | 50 ft |
| Commercial/flat roof scuppers | 6"+ box or custom | 4x4 or 4x5 | Per design |

### Gutter Fittings

- **End caps**: 2 per run (left + right), unless run terminates at a corner/miter
- **Inside/outside miters**: At every corner. Inside corners collect debris — recommend cleaning access.
- **Outlet drops**: 1 per downspout location. Connects gutter to downspout.
- **Expansion joints**: 1 per 40 LF of continuous gutter run. Critical in climates with large temperature swings.
- **Hidden hangers**: Every 24" OC (standard), 18" OC in snow country
- **Downspout elbows**: 3 per downspout (2 at top to offset from fascia, 1 at bottom)
- **Downspout straps**: 2 per story per downspout

### Flat Roof Drainage (Scuppers)

- **Scupper sizing**: Minimum 4" wide x 4" tall for primary. 6"+ for larger roof areas.
- **Scupper placement**: At low points of the roof, typically every 20-30 LF of parapet.
- **Collector boxes**: Transition from scupper to downspout. Typically custom-fabricated sheet metal.
- **Emergency overflow**: Scupper at 2" above primary, or through-wall overflow. Required by code.
- **Downspout from scupper**: Same sizing as conventional gutters. Route away from foundation.

## Upsell Opportunities

- **Impact-resistant shingles (Class 4)**: In Denver/Front Range, this is almost mandatory for insurance reasons. 15-30% insurance discount on a $2,000-3,000/year policy pays for the upgrade in 2-3 years. Cost impact: +$20-30/square. Pitch: "Pays for itself through insurance savings in under 3 years."
- **Gutter guards/leaf protection**: Reduces maintenance from twice-yearly to once every 3-5 years. Cost impact: $3-8/LF depending on type (screen vs. micro-mesh). Pitch: "Eliminates the ladder twice a year. Micro-mesh keeps even pine needles out."
- **Oversized gutters (6" vs. 5")**: Denver's summer thunderstorms can dump heavy rain. 6" gutters handle 40% more flow than 5". Cost impact: +$1-2/LF. Pitch: "Handles the downpours that overwhelm standard gutters."
- **Seamless gutters**: Made on-site from continuous coil stock. No seams = no leak points. Cost impact: same or slightly more than sectional. Pitch: "The seams are where gutters fail — seamless eliminates that."
- **Snow guards (for metal roofs)**: Prevents avalanche of snow/ice off metal roofs onto walkways and landscaping. Required where roof discharges over entries, walkways, or lower roofs. Cost impact: $5-12/LF. Pitch: "Prevents a liability issue and protects landscaping."
- **Heated gutter cables**: In areas with ice dam risk, heat cables in the gutter and along the eave prevent ice buildup. Cost impact: $15-25/LF. Pitch: "Prevents ice dams at the source."

## Gotchas & Common Mistakes

- **Assuming gutters on flat roofs** — Flat roofs with parapets use scuppers or internal drains, not traditional gutters. Confirm the drainage design before estimating.
- **Forgetting expansion joints in long gutter runs** — Metal expands ~1/8" per 10 ft per 100F temperature change. A 50 LF run in Denver (80F swing) moves almost 1/2". Without expansion joints, gutters buckle or pull off the fascia.
- **Undersized downspouts** — 2x3 downspouts with 6" gutters bottleneck the system. Match downspout capacity to gutter size.
- **No splash blocks or downspout extensions** — Water dumped at the foundation causes settling and basement leaks. Every downspout needs a splash block or extension directing water 4+ feet from the foundation.
- **Nailing shingles through ice barrier** — Ice and water shield is self-sealing around nails, but only when warm. In cold weather installation, the sealing may be delayed. Not a defect, but explain to the customer if they see exposed nail heads.
- **Missing kick-out flashing** — Where a roof slope terminates at a sidewall, water runs off the bottom shingle directly into the wall. A kick-out flashing directs it into the gutter. This $15 piece of metal prevents thousands in water damage.
- **Ridge vent without adequate soffit intake** — Ridge vent alone creates negative pressure that pulls conditioned air from the living space. Soffit intake must match or exceed ridge vent exhaust.
