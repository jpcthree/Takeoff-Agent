## Code Minimums by Climate Zone

### IECC 2021 Residential Prescriptive (Table R402.1.2)

| Climate Zone | Ceiling | Wood Frame Wall | Mass Wall | Floor | Basement Wall | Slab R / Depth | Crawlspace |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | R-30 | R-13 | R-3/4 | R-13 | R-0 | R-0 | R-0 |
| 2 | R-38 | R-13 | R-4/6 | R-13 | R-0 | R-0 | R-0 |
| 3 | R-38 | R-20 or R-13+5ci | R-8/13 | R-19 | R-5ci or R-13 | R-0 | R-5ci or R-13 |
| 4 (except Marine) | R-49 | R-20 or R-13+5ci | R-8/13 | R-19 | R-10ci or R-13 | R-10, 2ft | R-10ci or R-13 |
| 5 | R-49 | R-20 or R-13+5ci | R-13/17 | R-30 | R-15ci or R-19 | R-10, 2ft | R-15ci or R-19 |
| 6 | R-49 | R-20+5ci or R-13+10ci | R-15/20 | R-30 | R-15ci or R-19 | R-10, 4ft | R-15ci or R-19 |
| 7-8 | R-49 | R-20+5ci or R-13+10ci | R-19/21 | R-38 | R-15ci or R-19 | R-10, 4ft | R-15ci or R-19 |

Notes:
- "ci" = continuous insulation. Continuous means uninterrupted by framing — reduces thermal bridging.
- First number in mass wall is interior, second is exterior.
- Denver (Zone 5B) requires R-49 ceiling, R-20 or R-13+5ci walls, R-30 floors, R-15ci or R-19 basement walls.

## Best Practices

- **Always insulate rim/band joists** — Even when plans don't call it out, rim joists are a massive air leakage and heat loss point. Closed-cell spray foam (R-6.5/inch) is the standard. 2" minimum for Zone 5+ gives R-13 and vapor/air barrier in one pass.
- **Continuous insulation on all above-grade exterior walls** — Breaks thermal bridging through studs. R-5 ci minimum in Zone 5, but R-10 is increasingly standard for high-performance homes.
- **Verify cavity depth matches insulation specification** — R-13 batt in a 2x4 wall is fine (3.5" cavity). R-15 batt needs minimum 3.5" too. But R-21 needs a 2x6 wall (5.5" cavity). Compressed batts lose R-value.
- **Seal penetrations before insulating** — Spray foam around electrical boxes, plumbing penetrations, and wire runs. This is an air sealing task but must happen during the insulation phase.
- **Bib & blow (blown-in-blanket)** — Superior to batts for irregular cavities, around wiring/plumbing. Uses fabric stapled over studs, then cellulose or fiberglass blown to full density. No gaps, no compression. Same R-value per inch as batts but better real-world performance.
- **Attic air sealing before blowing** — If the project has blown attic insulation, all penetrations in the attic floor must be sealed first. Top plates, electrical, plumbing, HVAC boots.
- **Garage ceiling insulation** — Required when living space is above. R-38 minimum in Zone 5. Also requires air sealing of the garage-to-house boundary (code-required separation).
- **Slab edge insulation** — Often missed on plans. Required in Zone 4+ for heated slabs and Zone 5+ for all slabs. Extends to footing depth or 2ft minimum.

## Common Alternatives

- **Blown cellulose vs. fiberglass batt (walls)**: Cellulose fills cavities completely, better sound attenuation, higher recycled content. Cost impact: +$0.05-0.15/SF installed. Best for: retrofit, irregular framing, sound-sensitive areas. Trade-off: requires fabric or netting installation, slightly slower.
- **Closed-cell spray foam vs. open-cell (roofs/walls)**: Closed-cell is vapor barrier + air barrier + insulation in one. R-6.5/inch vs R-3.7/inch for open-cell. Cost impact: closed-cell is ~2x the cost of open-cell. Best for: rim joists, cathedral ceilings, below-grade. Trade-off: cost, less vapor-permeable (can trap moisture if assembly gets wet from exterior).
- **Flash-and-batt hybrid**: 1-2" closed-cell spray foam on sheathing + fiberglass batt fill. Gets air seal + vapor control from spray foam, bulk R-value cheaply from batt. Cost impact: 15-25% less than full spray foam. Best for: Zone 5+ walls and cathedral ceilings where full spray foam is cost-prohibitive.
- **Mineral wool continuous insulation vs. rigid foam (XPS/EPS/polyiso)**: Mineral wool is vapor-permeable, non-combustible, dimensionally stable. Cost impact: +15-30% over EPS, comparable to polyiso. Best for: fire-sensitive assemblies, rain screen walls, high-temperature environments. Trade-off: heavier, harder to cut, needs furring strips for cladding attachment.
- **GPS (graphite polystyrene) vs. EPS**: GPS has ~20% better R-value per inch than standard EPS (R-4.7 vs R-3.9). Cost impact: +10-15% over EPS. Best for: when you need more R-value without increasing thickness. Trade-off: slightly more expensive.

## Climate Zone Considerations

### Zone 5 (Cold — Denver, Salt Lake City, Boise)

- **Vapor retarder required on interior side of exterior walls** — Kraft-faced batts satisfy this. If using unfaced batts + separate vapor retarder, verify it's on the warm side.
- **Dew point control critical** — If using exterior continuous insulation, at least 35% of total wall R-value must be exterior to keep sheathing above dew point. Example: R-13 cavity + R-7 ci is the minimum safe ratio.
- **Slab edge insulation mandatory** — R-10 minimum to 2ft depth. Denver typically requires R-10 to footing.
- **Cathedral/flat roof assemblies** — Unvented roof assemblies in Zone 5 per IRC R806.5 require air-impermeable insulation (spray foam) on the underside of the roof sheathing, OR the full R-value as air-impermeable. Hybrid approach (spray + blown) is common and cost-effective.
- **Altitude factors (Denver at 5,280 ft)** — Spray foam yields may differ slightly at altitude. Confirm coverage rates with installer. The dry climate means less moisture risk in wall assemblies but doesn't eliminate the need for vapor management.
- **Basement walls in Denver** — Below-grade walls stay cooler year-round. Interior continuous insulation (rigid foam or mineral wool) + stud wall with batt is standard. Avoid vapor barriers on the interior of below-grade walls — they trap moisture from the concrete.

### Zone 4 (Mixed — DC, Nashville, St. Louis)

- **Vapor retarder rules are nuanced** — Class III (latex paint) is acceptable with the right wall assembly. Avoid poly sheeting — it traps summer moisture drive.
- **Less exterior insulation needed** — R-5 ci meets code for most wall assemblies.
- **Crawlspace insulation** — Can insulate either the crawlspace walls (R-10 ci) or the floor above (R-19). Wall insulation + sealed crawlspace is usually more effective.

### Zone 6-7 (Very Cold — Minneapolis, Burlington, Helena)

- **Higher wall R-value required** — R-20+5ci or R-13+10ci. Double-stud walls become cost-competitive at these R-values.
- **Slab edge to 4ft depth** — Deeper frost line means more exposed slab edge.
- **Triple-pane windows start to make economic sense** above Zone 6.

## Upsell Opportunities

- **Spray foam upgrade at rim joists**: Even if walls get batts, upgrading rim joists to closed-cell spray foam is the single highest-ROI insulation improvement. Adds ~$500-800 for a typical home. Eliminates the #1 air leakage point. Pitch: "This is where most of your heating dollars escape."
- **Continuous exterior insulation**: If plans call for R-13 cavity only, suggest adding R-5 ci (mineral wool or rigid foam). Meets prescriptive code, breaks thermal bridging, adds ~$1.50-2.50/SF. Pitch: "Wrapping the house in a thermal blanket — your walls are only as warm as their weakest point."
- **Dense-pack cellulose over fiberglass batt**: $0.10-0.20/SF premium for noticeably better comfort (no drafts, better sound). Pitch: "Fills every gap and wire run — no cold spots."
- **Sound insulation in interior walls**: Plans often skip this. Adding R-13 batt in bedroom walls, bathroom walls, and home office walls costs ~$0.55/SF and dramatically improves privacy. Pitch: "Most homeowners wish they'd done this after move-in."
- **Garage ceiling air sealing + insulation**: Even if not living space above, insulating and air-sealing the garage ceiling reduces fumes and temperature transfer. Required by code when living space is above. Pitch: "Keeps garage fumes out and conditioned air in."
- **Attic hatch insulation kit**: Often overlooked. R-value of the attic access must match the surrounding insulation. Pre-made insulated hatch covers run $50-100. Pitch: "Your attic hatch is a hole in your insulation blanket."

## Gotchas & Common Mistakes

- **Compressed batts lose R-value** — R-19 batt compressed into a 2x4 cavity (3.5") loses ~25% of its R-value. Always match batt thickness to cavity depth.
- **Vapor barrier on wrong side** — In heating climates (Zone 5+), vapor retarder goes on the interior (warm) side. Putting poly on the exterior traps moisture in the wall and causes rot.
- **Missing rim joist insulation** — Plans frequently omit this. Code requires the same R-value as the above-grade wall. Always include it in the estimate.
- **Mixing insulation types carelessly** — Spray foam over kraft-faced batts creates a double vapor barrier. If using spray foam, use unfaced batts or remove facing.
- **Forgetting slab edge insulation** — Required in Zone 4+ but often missing from plans. Check and include in estimate.
- **Insulation over recessed lights** — Non-IC-rated fixtures need 3" clearance. IC-rated airtight fixtures can be covered. Verify fixture type before insulating over them.
- **Not accounting for framing factor** — Standard framing factor is 15-25% of wall area. When using R-value of the cavity only, the whole-wall R-value is lower. Continuous insulation addresses this.
