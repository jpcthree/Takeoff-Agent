## Vapor Drive Rules

The fundamental rule: vapor moves from warm/moist to cold/dry.

- **Zones 1-3 (Cooling dominant)**: Vapor drive is inward in summer. Avoid interior vapor barriers. Use vapor-permeable interior finishes. Class III vapor retarder or none on interior.
- **Zone 4 (Mixed)**: Vapor drive reverses seasonally. Use "smart" vapor retarders (variable permeance) or rely on latex paint as Class III retarder. Avoid polyethylene on either side.
- **Zones 5-8 (Heating dominant)**: Vapor drive is outward in winter. Class I or II vapor retarder on warm side (interior). Kraft-faced batts or polyethylene acceptable. Exterior sheathing must be vapor-permeable OR use enough exterior insulation to keep sheathing above dew point.

### Dew Point Control — Exterior Insulation Ratios (Zones 5-8)

To prevent condensation on the back of exterior sheathing, a minimum percentage of total R-value must be outboard of the sheathing:

| Zone | Min. Exterior R Ratio | Example: R-20 wall needs |
|------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| 5 | 35% | R-7 continuous exterior |
| 6 | 41% | R-8.2 continuous exterior |
| 7 | 46% | R-9.2 continuous exterior |
| 8 | 51% | R-10.2 continuous exterior |

Reference: IRC R702.7.1

## Air Sealing Priorities

Air leakage accounts for 25-40% of heating/cooling energy loss. Priority sealing locations (highest impact first):

1. **Rim/band joists** — Largest single source. Spray foam is gold standard.
2. **Attic/roof penetrations** — Plumbing vents, electrical, can lights, HVAC chases
3. **Window/door rough openings** — Low-expansion foam or backer rod + caulk
4. **Sill plate to foundation** — Sill seal + caulk
5. **Electrical boxes on exterior walls** — Airtight boxes or foam pads
6. **Recessed lighting** — IC-rated airtight fixtures or covers above
7. **Duct penetrations through conditioned envelope**

IECC 2021 requires air leakage testing: **3 ACH50 max** for Zones 3-8, **5 ACH50 max** for Zones 1-2.

## Thermal Bridging

Steel studs reduce effective R-value by 40-60%. Wood studs reduce by 10-15%.

**Solutions ranked by effectiveness:**
1. Continuous exterior insulation (eliminates bridging entirely)
2. Staggered-stud or double-stud walls (interrupts framing thermal path)
3. Insulated headers and corners (reduces point bridging)
4. Thermal break strips at studs (modest improvement)

## Altitude Considerations (5,000+ ft)

- **Spray foam**: Blowing agents behave differently at altitude. Closed-cell foam may have slightly different yield. Verify with installer.
- **Drying potential**: Low humidity at altitude means faster drying of moisture in assemblies — beneficial for wall performance.
- **UV exposure**: Higher UV at altitude degrades exposed materials faster. House wrap, foam board, and roofing membranes need faster coverage after installation.
- **Temperature swings**: Mountain climates see 30-40F daily swings. Expansion/contraction of materials (especially metal flashing, gutters) is accelerated.

## Energy Code Compliance Paths

### IECC 2021 Residential

1. **Prescriptive (R402)** — Meet or exceed table values for insulation, windows, air sealing
2. **Total UA Alternative (R402.1.5)** — Trade off between components as long as total heat loss doesn't exceed prescriptive baseline
3. **Simulated Performance (R405)** — Energy model shows proposed design uses less energy than standard reference design
4. **ERI (Energy Rating Index) (R406)** — HERS-like score must be below zone threshold. Most flexible path. Allows trade-offs between envelope, HVAC, renewables.

**Denver 2025 amendments** adopt IECC 2021 with local modifications including mandatory EV-ready wiring and electrification-ready provisions.
