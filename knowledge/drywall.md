# Drywall Knowledge Base

> Last updated: 2026-03-27
> Sources: IRC 2021, GA-214 (Gypsum Association), GA-600, UL Fire Resistance Directory, USG Technical Library

## Code Minimums

### Fire-Rated Assemblies (IRC R302)

| Location | Requirement | Board Type | Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Garage-to-house separation (walls) | 1-hour fire rating | 5/8" Type X both sides, or 5/8" Type X garage side | IRC R302.6 |
| Garage-to-house separation (ceiling) | 1-hour fire rating (if living space above) | 5/8" Type X | IRC R302.6 |
| Attached garage ceiling (no living above) | 1/2" gypsum minimum | 1/2" regular | IRC R302.6 |
| Townhouse/duplex party wall | 1-hour fire rating minimum | 5/8" Type X each side | IRC R302.2 |
| Furnace/water heater closet | Not required unless rated assembly | 1/2" regular typical | Local codes vary |
| Under-stair storage | 1/2" gypsum on enclosure | 1/2" regular | IRC R302.7 |

### Moisture-Resistant Board Requirements

| Location | Requirement | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Bathrooms (around tub/shower) | Moisture-resistant board or cement board | IRC R702.3.8 |
| Behind tile in wet areas | Cement board or equivalent | IRC R702.4 |
| Kitchen backsplash areas | Moisture-resistant recommended (not required by code) | Best practice |
| Laundry rooms | Moisture-resistant recommended | Best practice |
| Below-grade walls | Mold-resistant board recommended | Best practice |

### GA-214 Finish Levels

| Level | Description | Typical Use | Labor Multiplier |
|---|---|---|---|
| L0 | No finishing. Tape/fastener coverage not required. | Concealed areas, above ceilings, plenums | 0.0x |
| L1 | All joints and interior angles taped, one coat compound. Tool marks and ridges acceptable. | Concealed areas where fire rating requires tape | 0.3x |
| L2 | All joints spotted + one separate coat. Accessories covered one coat. | Substrate for tile, behind cabinets, garage interiors | 0.5x |
| L3 | Two separate coats on joints, one on accessories. Sufficient for medium-heavy texture. | Areas receiving heavy texture | 0.7x |
| L4 | Three coats on joints, two on accessories. Smooth to touch with no tool marks. | Standard for flat paint, light texture, wallpaper | 1.0x (baseline) |
| L5 | Level 4 + skim coat entire surface. No imperfections visible under any lighting. | Critical lighting areas, high-gloss paint, dark colors | 1.5x |

## Best Practices

- **Back-blocking at butt joints** — Butt joints (non-tapered ends) are the #1 source of visible seams. Back-blocking or using butt-joint finishing strips eliminates the ridge. Worth the extra labor on any L4+ finish.
- **Hang ceilings before walls** — Industry standard. Wall sheets support ceiling edges and create a tighter fit. Reduces visible gaps at wall-ceiling intersection.
- **Fire-rated assemblies need setting-type compound on first coat** — All-purpose compound shrinks and can crack, compromising the fire rating. Setting-type (hot mud) doesn't shrink. Required by UL for listed assemblies.
- **Screw pattern matters for fire ratings** — Type X assemblies typically require screws at 12" OC on ceilings and 16" OC on walls. Standard is 16" OC walls, 12" OC ceilings. Verify per UL listing.
- **Control joints in long runs** — Drywall runs over 30 feet should have a control joint to prevent cracking from building movement. ASTM C840 recommends every 30 feet.
- **Moisture-resistant board is NOT waterproof** — Green board (moisture-resistant) handles humidity but not direct water contact. Behind showers and tubs, use cement board or a waterproof membrane system.
- **Abuse-resistant board in high-traffic areas** — Garages, mudrooms, hallways, and kids' rooms benefit from abuse-resistant (impact-resistant) board. Harder surface resists dents and holes.
- **Stagger joints between layers in multi-layer assemblies** — For 2-layer fire-rated assemblies, offset the joints by at least 12" between layers. Required by most UL listings.
- **Airtight drywall approach (ADA)** — Using drywall as the primary air barrier. Requires gaskets at top/bottom plates, caulk at all penetrations, airtight electrical boxes. Cost-effective alternative to spray foam air sealing.

## Common Alternatives

- **5/8" Type X everywhere (vs. 1/2" standard)**: Some builders use 5/8" Type X as the standard throughout. Stiffer, better sound, fire-rated by default. Cost impact: +$3-4/sheet (+25%). Best for: spec homes, builders who want one SKU on site. Trade-off: heavier, harder on installers.
- **Mold-resistant board (purple board) throughout**: Instead of using moisture-resistant only in wet areas, some builders use mold-resistant everywhere. Cost impact: +$4-6/sheet. Best for: humid climates, below-grade, rental properties. Trade-off: cost, usually overkill in dry climates.
- **Level 5 finish in living areas**: Plans typically call for L4. Upgrading main living spaces to L5 (skim coat) eliminates any visible imperfections under critical lighting. Cost impact: +$0.35-0.50/SF (labor). Best for: modern designs with lots of natural light, dark paint colors. Trade-off: significant labor increase.
- **Sound-rated assemblies (STC 50+)**: Standard interior wall is STC 33-35. Adding insulation + resilient channel or double layer brings it to STC 50+. Cost impact: +$2-4/SF. Best for: bedrooms, bathrooms, home offices, media rooms. Trade-off: cost, slightly thicker wall.
- **Paperless drywall**: Fiberglass-faced instead of paper-faced. Immune to mold growth on the facer. Cost impact: +$3-5/sheet. Best for: basements, flood-prone areas. Trade-off: harder to finish (fiberglass face needs specific compound).

## Climate Zone Considerations

### Zone 5 (Cold — Denver)

- **Vapor retarder coordination** — In Zone 5, the drywall itself can serve as the vapor retarder if painted with vapor-retarder paint. Alternatively, use kraft-faced batts behind the drywall. Do NOT add poly behind drywall if the wall has exterior foam insulation — creates a double vapor barrier.
- **Airtight drywall approach works well** — Denver's dry climate means less moisture in the air to manage. ADA with gaskets and caulk is a cost-effective air sealing strategy.
- **Dry climate = faster finishing** — Joint compound dries faster in Denver's low humidity. Good for schedule but requires attention — compound can dry too fast in summer, causing poor adhesion. Mist lightly in hot/dry conditions.

### Zone 2-3 (Hot-Humid — Houston, Atlanta)

- **Mold-resistant board everywhere is justified** — High humidity makes paper-faced drywall a mold risk even in non-wet areas. The cost premium is insurance.
- **Vapor retarder on exterior side** — Opposite of cold climates. Do NOT put poly behind interior drywall in humid climates.

### Zone 6-7 (Very Cold — Minneapolis)

- **Vapor retarder is critical** — Use Class I or II on the warm (interior) side. Poly is acceptable here.
- **Consider smart vapor retarders** — MemBrain or similar variable-permeance products allow drying in both directions seasonally.

## Upsell Opportunities

- **Sound insulation in bedroom/bathroom walls**: Plans rarely specify this for single-family homes, but homeowners consistently wish they'd done it. R-13 batt + resilient channel in bedroom walls costs ~$2.50/SF additional and raises STC from 33 to ~50. Pitch: "You'll hear the difference the first night."
- **Abuse-resistant board in garage**: Standard drywall in garages gets dinged by car doors, bikes, and tools within months. Abuse-resistant board costs ~$4/sheet more and handles the abuse. Pitch: "Saves you a callback in year one."
- **Level 5 finish at feature walls and stairwells**: Full skim coat where light rakes across walls. Prevents the "shadow lines" visible at joints under natural light. Adds ~$0.40/SF. Pitch: "The difference between a good drywall job and an invisible one."
- **Mold-resistant board in basements**: Even in dry climates, below-grade spaces can experience moisture events. Mold-resistant board costs $4-6/sheet more and provides peace of mind. Pitch: "Insurance against the water heater that eventually leaks."
- **Access panels at plumbing shutoffs**: Plans often forget these. Every tub/shower valve needs accessible shutoff. 12x12 access panel is $18 installed vs. cutting open the wall later. Pitch: "Code requires access to shutoffs — this is how you do it without a drywall repair."
- **Pre-rock blocking for TV mounts, shelving, grab bars**: If the framing plan doesn't include blocking for common wall-mount items, suggesting it during the drywall phase saves the homeowner from toggle bolts or surface-mount solutions later. Pitch: "Once the drywall's up, adding backing is a $300 repair. Now it's a $15 piece of plywood."

## Gotchas & Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting garage-to-house fire separation** — IRC R302.6 requires 1/2" or 5/8" Type X on the garage side of shared walls and ceiling. This is one of the most common code violations in residential construction.
- **Using regular compound on fire-rated joints** — All-purpose mud is fine for non-rated walls but setting-type compound is required on fire-rated assemblies per UL listings. The inspector will catch this.
- **Not enough screws on ceiling board** — Gravity works against ceiling drywall. 12" OC on ceilings (vs. 16" on walls). 5/8" board on ceilings with 24" OC joists tends to sag — use 1/2" on 16" OC or 5/8" on 24" OC.
- **Mixing up moisture-resistant and mold-resistant** — Green board (moisture-resistant) resists moisture in the paper. Purple/mold-resistant board has anti-microbial treatment. They're different products at different price points.
- **Hanging drywall horizontally vs. vertically** — Horizontal is standard for walls 9' and under (fewer joints, stronger at mid-wall where impacts happen). Vertical for walls over 9' to minimize butt joints.
- **Forgetting the ceiling drywall type in fire-rated rooms** — When a garage has living space above, the garage ceiling needs 5/8" Type X. Easy to miss if the room schedule only notes wall types.
